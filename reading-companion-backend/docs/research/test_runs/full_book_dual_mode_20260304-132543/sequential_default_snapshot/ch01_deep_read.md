# Chapter 1

## Section 1.2: Prosocial behavior and complementary needs

💡 Highlight

> "to want has a double meaning: it can mean both to desire and to lack. This is why people who want the same things are generally useless to each other: each lacks what the other desires."

This is the kind of efficient, non-sentimental insight the author promised in the preface. It reframes a common platitude ('opposites attract') into something with actual logical grounding. The double meaning of 'want' is linguistically elegant - though I wonder if it holds across languages, which would affect how universal the argument is.

⚡ Discern

> "It is neither the good nor the loving nor the virtuous who are desired for relationships, but..."

The author sets up a deliberate contrast: he explicitly rejects 'the good, the loving, the virtuous' as the basis for relationship desirability - but then cuts off. This is classic structural hooksmanship, and it works. But I'm already suspicious: is this just contrarian clickbait, or will the actual answer be genuinely illuminating? The passage asks me to hold judgment until the next section delivers.

🔗 Connect Back

> "And this explains – all by itself – why certain people are rich in relationship opportunities and others are not."

This connects to the title wordplay the author discussed earlier ('worthiness' vs 'price'). Here the 'price' reading dominates: relationships as transactional exchange of complementary wants. The earlier promise that 'both are true' is being tested - this section leans heavily into the cynical frame. I'm curious whether the book will eventually acknowledge the 'worthiness' side, or whether the author genuinely believes complementarity explains everything about relationship opportunities.

## Section 1.5: Relationships as games with rules and goals

💡 Highlight

> "A game is anything with rules and a goal."

This is the operational definition the author needs to preempt the obvious objection. It's efficient: not defending 'game' as a casual/trivial concept, but redefining it entirely. The move is clean but also reveals the author's rhetorical strategy - anticipate resistance and reframe before it lands.

💡 Highlight

> "The game of friendship with a specific individual is different from the game of friendship with any other individual."

This nested structure is the real contribution here, not the game metaphor itself. The insight that even friendships of the same 'type' differ because people want different things from different friends - this is where the earlier double meaning of 'want' (desire + lack) gets operationalized.

⚡ Discern

> "If people get too little of what they want (or too much of what they don't want), they stop playing"

This sounds too clean. People stay in unsatisfactory relationships, jobs, friendships all the time. The 'stop playing' claim is determinism dressed as observation. What's missing: what makes someone tolerate imbalance? The author might be describing an ideal-typical response rather than an empirical one, but he doesn't flag that distinction.

🔗 Connect Back

> "people don't want the exact same set of things from all their friends"

This is the earlier 'want = desire + lack' insight now made concrete. Different friends fill different lacks, so different relationships have different goal-structures even within the same category. This connects 7.5 back to the conceptual foundation laid in 7.3.

## Section 1.9: Value definition and resource expenditure

💡 Highlight

> "Value is something that people are willing to pay for. And payment, in turn, is the expenditure of resources."

Clean operational definition. 'Willing to pay for' shifts the frame from intrinsic worth to demonstrated preference - behavior over stated values. The expansion to include time, effort, attention, and opportunity is crucial: it prevents the model from collapsing into crude 'money-for-sex' transactionalism and opens space for the full range of what people actually invest in relationships.

💡 Highlight

> "The covert calculator"

This section header is doing serious work. It frames the entire inquiry in one memorable image - not 'the rational actor' (cold, abstract) but 'covert' (hidden, operative beneath awareness). This is the rhetorical bridge between the economic model and lived emotional experience: the calculator isn't gone, it's just invisible to the person doing the calculating.

💡 Highlight

> "resources – once expended – cannot be refunded."

The final sentence carries moral weight. It's not just an economic statement - it's existential. Time spent on one relationship is time not spent on another. Attention given to one person is attention withdrawn from others. The irreversibility is what makes the 'covert calculator' consequential, not just descriptive.

⚡ Discern

> "the model can explain emotions (but emotions cannot explain the model), this means that the model is more fundamentally true and should take precedence."

This is a significant logical move that deserves scrutiny. The author claims explanatory asymmetry (model explains emotions, not vice versa) implies ontological primacy ('more fundamentally true'). But this conflates two different claims: (1) the model is a useful framework for understanding emotions, and (2) the model is 'more true' than emotional experience. Explanatory power doesn't automatically trump lived experience as a criterion of truth - especially when the thing being explained IS the experience. This is where the argument shifts from 'useful model' to 'fundamental reality,' and the move isn't fully defended.

✍️ Association

> "Value is something that people are willing to pay for."

This echoes the earlier 'want = desire + lack' insight: if value is what you're willing to pay for, then different people paying different 'prices' (in various resources) implies they want different things. The economic model gives operational content to the linguistic observation about 'want' having double meaning. Different wants = different costs = different relationships. The framework is starting to cohere.

## Section 1.10: Drivers of value fluctuation

💡 Highlight

> "This is essentially why the gods in Greek mythology envied man: mortality (i.e., scarce time) made his life valuable."

This is the kind of compact, memorable insight the author does well. The Greek mythology reference isn't decorative - it distills the entire scarcity-creates-value argument into one sentence. Mortality as the source of value is a genuinely striking reframing.

💡 Highlight

> "People might scoff at paying ten dollars for a bottle of water – but a man dying of thirst would be willing to pay a great deal more. But he will likely not be willing to pay as much for a second bottle..."

The thirst example is the payoff for this section. It makes the abstract framework (goal-relevance + information) concrete and shows the dynamic the author promised: same person, same good, different moment = different value. This is marginal utility rendered as narrative.

⚡ Discern

> "Objective reality, on the other hand, can't be wrong (or right, for that matter): it simply is what it is."

This is a strong philosophical claim that sits oddly with the rest of the argument. If value is purely subjective (as the author argues), then by what logic does he claim objective reality 'simply is what it is'? That's itself a claim about reality that could be right or wrong. The distinction he's drawing (subjectivity can be 'wrong,' objectivity 'simply is') needs more support - it feels like he's smuggling in a metaphysical position without defending it.

⚡ Discern

> "There are two primary drivers of value fluctuation at the individual level: goal-relevance and information."

'Primary' and 'two' are doing a lot of work here. The author states this as definitive fact, but what about other drivers? Emotional states, social context, temporal mood, physiological condition? The certainty feels premature for a text that claims to be a 'survey.' This could be framework-building that will be refined later, but the bald assertion doesn't match the hedging we saw earlier.

🔗 Connect Back

> "the more we perceive a specific good to be instrumental to achieving a personally relevant goal... the more we invest that specific good with value"

This gives operational content to the 'covert calculator' metaphor from 7.9. The calculator isn't just adding up costs - it's dynamically weighting goods based on how instrumental they appear to current goals. The thirst example shows this recalculation happening in real time as information changes (body signals first bottle need vs. second bottle need). This transforms the model from mechanical to alive.

🔗 Connect Back

> "the game of, say, making a presentation is embedded in the game of a job, which is embedded in the game of money, which is embedded in the game of society, which is embedded in the game of survival"

This nested hierarchy gives structural depth to the simple 'game = rules + goal' definition from 7.5. Games aren't just parallel; they're vertically ordered, and higher-level games (survival) take precedence over lower-level ones (presentation). The fire example makes this vivid: perceive the building is on fire → survival game suddenly dominant → manager's disapproval becomes irrelevant. This explains WHY value fluctuates: information shifts which game is salient.

## Section 1.11: Value determination process

💡 Highlight

> "the principles of valuation are like the principles of logic: they do not change from place to place or moment to moment. What's more, neither can be empirically proven: we just somehow perceive them to be self-evidently true."

This is the boldest claim in the chapter so far - equating valuation principles with logical axioms. The comparison is seductive but problematic: logical principles are normative (how we *should* reason), while valuation tendencies are descriptive (how we *do* reason). The author conflates these two categories.

⚡ Discern

> "there is no culture in which it doesn't exist. This is because the principles of valuation are like the principles of logic"

The universality claim needs far more support than a assertion. Different cultures do not weight the same factors identically - the 'likelihood of success' might be universal, but the entire valuation framework (which goods are even being evaluated, what counts as 'success,' what goals are salient) is culturally mediated. The author conflates 'universal tendency to weigh probability' with 'universal valuation framework.' These are very different claims.

⚡ Discern

> "Neither can be empirically proven: we just somehow perceive them to be self-evidently true."

This is a category error. The author is making empirical claims about human psychology (how people actually calculate value) while simultaneously claiming these principles 'cannot be empirically proven.' But the entire argument *is* empirical observation dressed in the language of logical axioms. You can't have it both ways: either these are transcendental logical principles (in which case they're not about human psychology), or they're empirical generalizations about human behavior (in which case they can be tested and potentially falsified).

💡 Highlight

> "the output of an extremely complex calculation comprised of a countless number of evaluations mediated by perception, memory, and imagination... calculated anew hundreds of times every second for an uncountable number of goods simultaneously"

This is the operational payoff for the 'covert calculator' metaphor from 7.9. The author is showing the actual complexity beneath the metaphor - it's not just comparing prices, but simultaneously running dozens of parallel calculations across multiple goods, multiple methods, multiple goals. This makes the model feel much more alive and dynamic than simple cost-benefit analysis.

🔗 Connect Back

> "the principles of valuation... are hardwired into our brains"

This extends the move in 7.10 where the author shifted from 'useful model' to 'fundamental reality.' Now he's claiming the valuation faculty is as basic as logical reasoning - hardwired, not learned. This connects to the earlier tension: the author keeps making this ontological leap (model → reality) without fully defending it. The hardwiring claim is stronger than anything he's established so far.

## Section 1.12: The covert calculator mechanism

💡 Highlight

> "The issue is that almost no one experiences the determination of value in this way. When presented like this, the valuation process feels alien or robotic or sociopathic."

This is the paragraph where the mask slips. The author spends pages building an elegant economic model of human relationships, then suddenly admits that when you describe it accurately, people find it repulsive. That's a remarkable moment of honesty - he's essentially saying 'this is true, but it sounds monstrous.' The question is: why does it sound monstrous? What is the model missing?

⚡ Discern

> "almost no one experiences the determination of value in this way"

The author treats this as an observation about human psychology (we're unaware of our own valuation processes), but it might also be a critique of the model itself. If no one recognizes their own experience in an allegedly universal model, that's not just a limitation of human introspection - it might mean the model is missing something essential about what valuation actually IS in lived experience. The author doesn't consider this reading.

🔗 Connect Back

> "the model can explain emotions (but emotions cannot explain the model)"

The 'alien or robotic' reaction is the experiential limit that undermines the earlier claim. If the model explains emotions but makes them feel inhuman when articulated, that's not a minor inconvenience - it suggests the model might be explaining something OTHER than what emotions feel like from the inside. The author built explanatory power but lost phenomenological validity, and he doesn't seem to notice the cost.

✍️ Association

> "This is because the vast majority of this process occurs well below the threshold of awareness"

This reminds me of Kahneman's System 1/System 2 distinction, but with an important twist: Kahneman presents fast thinking as intuitive and normal, whereas this author presents the covert calculator as secretly computational in a way that feels dystopian when exposed. The framing matters - 'covert' carries a slightly sinister connotation that 'automatic' or 'intuitive' doesn't.

## Section 1.16: Goal conflation and trade-offs

💡 Highlight

> "The cruel reality is that she will have become less valuable to him for having given him what he wanted."

This is the passage's sharpest articulation of the paradox: achieving a goal makes the person who helped achieve it less valuable. The mechanism (goal completion reduces goal-rank) was implicit in earlier sections, but this explicit statement - giving someone what they want makes you less valuable to them - is the uncomfortable truth that justifies the entire framework. The plumber analogy ('no one continues to pay the plumber') completes a thought that was foreshadowed earlier.

💡 Highlight

> "there aren't any solutions, only trade-offs"

This phrasing is doing real work. It's not just saying relationships involve compromise - it's claiming that the existence of multiple goals within a single relationship creates an inherently unsolvable structure. Every choice is a loss of something else, always. That's a stark worldview.

💡 Highlight

> "What’s more, the individual is generally only made aware of the outcome of this process when the value coefficient is transmuted into an emotion. And with respect to sexual relationships, the emotion into which this value coefficient is transmuted is desire."

This is the mechanism connecting the abstract valuation framework to lived experience. Desire as the visible tip of an invisible calculation - this is what makes the model feel both compelling and uncomfortable. We're not aware we're calculating; we only feel the result as wanting.

🔗 Connect Back

> "And this brings us to the second significant complicator: lack of awareness."

This reinforces the tension from 7.13: the valuation process is real but people reject it when articulated honestly. The 'alien or robotic' admission there (noted in my earlier reactions) now gets named as 'lack of awareness' - a softer framing that makes the framework more defensible. The author is acknowledging the visceral resistance while asking the reader to push through it.

⚡ Discern

> "This is not only because the woman can no longer help him achieve one of his personally relevant goals (i.e., raising children) but also because this goal is no longer highly ranked (for having been achieved)."

The framework predicts relationship decline is inevitable once shared goals are achieved. But this is a strong empirical claim dressed as logical deduction. What about relationships that strengthen over time? What about couples who create new goals together? The model seems to assume goals are fixed and depletable, but humans are goal-generating creatures. This might be a case where the elegant logic overfits to a particular (potentially pessimistic) observation about relationships.

## Section 1.17: Approach-avoidance conflict

💡 Highlight

> "value and desire are the same thing experienced in different ways"

This is the philosophical heart of the chapter. The tautology the author identifies ('people desire a desirable individual') isn't a flaw—it's the point. Desire isn't something added to value; it's value experienced from the inside. This reframes the entire inquiry: we're not asking 'why do people desire valuable things?' but rather 'what does it feel like to perceive value in the domain of sexual relationships?' The answer is: it feels like desire.

💡 Highlight

> "In the context of sexual relationships, our emotional response to the perception of a high-value individual is desire... our emotional response to the perception of a low-value individual is disgust... our emotional response to the perception of a mid-value individual depends on the number and intensity of the benefits and liabilities involved."

This is the synthesis moment—the three-value mapping pulls together the framework from 7.12-7.16 into a single descriptive grid. What makes it elegant: desire and disgust aren't opposites on a spectrum but separate output channels. Mid-value is where it gets interesting because it doesn't produce a third emotion—it produces either indifference OR conflict depending on intensity.

💡 Highlight

> "This is called an approach-avoidance conflict, and it can trap people in agonizing indecision for years."

The name 'approach-avoidance conflict' is doing heavy lifting here—it transforms a descriptive emotional state into a recognized psychological phenomenon with clinical weight. 'Agonizing indecision for years' gives it existential stakes. This is where the abstract value framework becomes viscerally relatable: everyone has experienced being drawn to and repulsed by the same person.

⚡ Discern

> "we feel powerfully drawn to and repulsed by the same individual simultaneously"

The author frames desire and disgust as 'orthogonal'—two independent systems that can both be active. But this raises a question: is this actually how emotions work? Psychologically, emotions are often understood as transient, valenced states—not simultaneous independent activations. The author's framing sounds more like a computational model (two separate variables can both be non-zero) than a phenomenological description of what it actually feels like to be conflicted about someone. What does simultaneous desire-and-disgust feel like from the inside? The author assumes we know but doesn't describe it.

⚡ Discern

> "and it can trap people in agonizing indecision for years"

The 'years' claim is doing significant persuasive work but has no evidentiary support in the text. This is a bold empirical assertion dressed as description. Without evidence, it reads as rhetorical flourish meant to heighten the stakes of the framework. Is this observation from clinical practice? From the author's experience? From research? The reader can't evaluate this claim.

🔗 Connect Back

> "if we perceive that a person might be able to give us a lot of what we really want (and a lot of what we really don’t), then we typically feel conflicted"

This directly extends 7.16's analysis of goal conflation and trade-offs. When multiple goals are wrapped into a single relationship, and those goals have conflicting implications (one person satisfies Goal A beautifully but threatens Goal B), the 'mid-value' case isn't neutral—it's the site where competing goals within the same person generate the approach-avoidance conflict. The framework predicted this from earlier sections: the more goals conflated, the more potential for mixed signals.

🔗 Connect Back

> "The diminishment of his desire is the result of his (potentially unconscious) perception that his wife has become less valuable to him"

Returns to the core mechanism from 7.12-7.13: the covert calculator outputs value as emotion. The husband doesn't 'decide' to feel less attracted—he perceives his wife as less valuable given his evolved goal set, and desire diminishes as a direct consequence. The author explicitly notes he 'won't be able to prevent it.' This reinforces the determinism in the framework: desire isn't a choice, it's the phenomenological experience of having already calculated value.

✍️ Association

> "This is called an approach-avoidance conflict"

The clinical name 'approach-avoidance conflict' connects this personal relationship analysis to a well-established concept in psychology—originally from Kurt Lewin and later developed in anxiety research. What the author does is translate a general psychological phenomenon into a specific relational context: it's not just 'I'm torn between wanting and not wanting,' it's 'I'm torn between wanting this person and being disgusted by this person, and both responses are generated by the same value calculation.' That's a more precise mechanistic account than typical pop-psychology discussions of 'mixed feelings.'

## Section 1.18: Red flags and valuation bias

💡 Highlight

> "And this positive emotion will reciprocally influence their valuation process in predictable ways, namely: by weighting values associated with current benefits more heavily, and weighting values associated with future liabilities more lightly."

This is the section's key move: desire isn't just the output of valuation, it's an input that distorts future valuations. The word 'reciprocally' does all the work here - the author is saying valuation → desire → biased valuation → more desire. This transforms the earlier framework (value determines desire) into something more dynamic and darker. The person isn't just being attracted to a high-value individual; their attraction is actively making them worse at assessing that individual's future liabilities.

💡 Highlight

> "This causes people to (rationally) undervalue potential future liabilities, which then contributes to tipping their perception of any given individual in the direction of a net positive."

The word 'rationally' here is doing something interesting - it's claiming that systematic bias is actually rational. This is the same move we saw earlier where the author uses 'rational' to mean 'consistent with the goal of approach' rather than 'truth-tracking.' But there's an inherent tension: if you're trying to maximize long-term relationship satisfaction, systematically underweighting future liabilities is NOT rational. The author seems to mean 'rational given the motivational state of desire' rather than 'rational for accurate prediction.'

⚡ Discern

> "Ultimately, it is this reciprocal relationship between the perception of value and desire that explains why attractive people tend to receive all kinds of allowances that less attractive people don't – including the tendency to discount or minimize the implications of their bad behavior."

This universality claim mirrors the unsupported universality claims we flagged earlier. The author asserts that attractive people universally receive 'allowances' that less attractive people don't - but is this actually true across all contexts and cultures? The framework might explain why someone *would* give allowances, but whether they actually do, and in what magnitude, likely varies significantly based on cultural norms, individual differences in attachment style, the type of 'bad behavior' involved, and the relationship context. The certainty here feels overextended.

🔗 Connect Back

> "This causes people to (rationally) undervalue potential future liabilities"

This connects back to 7.17's approach-avoidance conflict. The author is now explaining the mechanism behind why the conflict gets resolved in favor of approach: when desire is activated, it systematically discounts the 'avoidance' side of the equation. The conflict isn't just experienced - it's actively tilted toward approach by the emotional state itself. This makes the approach-avoidance conflict more insidious than just 'feeling torn' - the desire itself is rigging the calculation.

🔗 Connect Back

> "weighting values associated with future liabilities more lightly"

This connects to the temporal discounting logic from earlier sections: the same liability is always less detrimental in the future than in the present because there's always a chance it won't materialize. The author is building a unified temporal framework - benefits are overweighted in the present, liabilities underweighted in the future. Both moves serve the same function: motivating approach behavior. This coherence across sections suggests the author has a genuinely unified model, not just post-hoc explanations.

## Section 1.19: Models fail due to lack of awareness

💡 Highlight

> "it is generally useless to directly ask people what they want in a sexual partner. Even if they could tell you the whole story (and they can't), they wouldn't't."

This is the passage's most consequential epistemological move. The author isn't just saying people are unaware of their values (the earlier point about unconscious processing) - he's making a stronger claim: even if people KNEW their values, they couldn't or wouldn't share them. The 'can't' and 'wouldn't' are doing different work: 'can't' refers to social unacceptability, 'wouldn't' refers to strategic self-protection. The job interviewer analogy crystallizes this perfectly.

⚡ Discern

> "Even if they could tell you the whole story (and they can't), they wouldn't."

There's a meta-epistemological problem here: if people can't report their values, and if social desirability distorts what they would report, how did the author himself come to understand this framework? He's essentially saying direct inquiry is useless - but what alternative methodology is available? The model explains behavior, but the author can't validate it through the very method (asking people) that would be most straightforward. This is a circularity: the framework's correctness relies on the very unreliability it predicts.

⚡ Discern

> "what people value – and how much they value those things – is not entirely biologically determined. It is both mediated by perception (which is not always reliable) and informed by culture"

This is a significant partial retreat from the hardwiring absolutism of earlier sections. Previously the author claimed valuation principles are like logical axioms - universal, unchanging, hardwired. Now he acknowledges cultural variability. But there's an internal tension: if perception is unreliable, what IS the valuation process tracking? The 'covert calculator' might be computing with fundamentally flawed data. The author doesn't grapple with what it would mean if the input to valuation (perception) is unreliable but the output (desire) feels certain.

🔗 Connect Back

> "Even if they could tell you the whole story (and they can't), they wouldn't."

This directly resolves the puzzle from 7.13-7.14 where the author admitted the model sounds 'alien or robotic' and people reject it when articulated honestly. Now we understand WHY: it's not just that people are unaware of their valuations (the unconscious processing point), it's that even conscious awareness wouldn't help because social desirability and strategic vulnerability prevent honest self-report. The model explains behavior, but people cannot recognize themselves in it - and now we know that's structurally guaranteed, not just a limitation of introspection.

🔗 Connect Back

> "As humans, we are born with the capacity to value, just like we are born with the capacity for language."

This extends the language analogy from earlier sections. In 7.10-7.11, the author claimed valuation principles are like logical axioms (universal, not learned). Now he's refining this: the CAPACITY is universal (like language capacity), but the CONTENT is learned (like particular languages). This is actually a more defensible position than the earlier hardwiring claims - but it significantly weakens the universality of the entire framework. If what's valued varies by culture, then the 'universal principles' become almost vacuous: everyone calculates, but they're calculating completely different things.

## Section 1.20: Training data quantity problems

💡 Highlight

> "The issue is that no one relationship, no matter the relationship, can serve as the exemplar for all relationships."

This is the section's thesis statement, and it's doing crucial work: acknowledging that variation exists while still maintaining that healthy relationships share structural features. The phrasing 'no one relationship, no matter the relationship' is almost a tongue-twister - the author is bending language to emphasize the universality of his否定.

💡 Highlight

> "We could call this the law of small numbers as applied to relationships, and it skews our valuations irrespective of the nature of the relationships on which they were trained."

The 'law of small numbers' borrowing from statistics is a smart rhetorical move - it imports scientific credibility to describe a phenomenon everyone intuitively understands. But there's something slightly worrying here: the law of small numbers in statistics refers to drawing faulty conclusions from insufficient sample sizes. Here it's being used to describe the formation of VALUES, not conclusions - a category shift that might not be warranted.

⚡ Discern

> "It is to say that there are many different ways for relationships to be healthy and functional (though, perhaps, not quite as many as there are ways for relationships to be unhealthy and dysfunctional)."

This empirical claim appears without any supporting evidence. How would one even count 'ways for relationships to be healthy'? This is the same pattern we saw earlier with 'years' of indecision - bold empirical assertions dressed as logical observations. The parenthetical 'perhaps' barely softens the claim. What would count as evidence for or against this? Comparative relationship research is notoriously difficult.

🔗 Connect Back

> "This is comparable to using one specific sparrow not only as the exemplar for all other sparrows but also for all other birds."

This extends the exemplar framework from 7.15 but with a crucial twist: there, exemplars were about perceived value of individuals. Here, exemplars are about the RELATIONSHIP TEMPLATE itself. The sparrow/condor/ostrich/condor cascade is memorable but slightly overwrought - birds are a natural kind, relationships are a social construct. The analogy might be doing too much work.

⚡ Discern

> "This is comparable to using one specific sparrow not only as the exemplar for all other sparrows but also for all other birds."

Wait - the author is making an implicit claim here: that people trained on one relationship template will fail to recognize OTHER healthy relationship templates as 'relationships at all.' That's a strong claim. Is it actually true that someone raised by loving parents can't recognize a bitter single-mother's relationship as a valid relationship? Or is the claim more subtle - that they'd recognize it but devalue it? The analogy conflates 'recognizing as bird' with 'valuing as exemplar,' which are different cognitive operations.

✍️ Association

> "children principally train their valuation algorithm on the data collected by observing the relationships of their primary caregivers."

This connects back to the earlier language analogy: we're born with the capacity to value (like language capacity), but we learn WHAT to value (like which language) from our environment. But there's a puzzle: if the training data is this constrained, how do first-generation only-children from single-parent households develop any functional relationship template at all? The author doesn't address where the initial training data comes from for the first generation.

## Section 1.21: Training data quality problems

💡 Highlight

> "This is because, if nothing else changes, this woman will still continue to desire exploitative men – even if she completely understands why she does so and why this desire is at cross-purposes with her best interests."

This is the passage's sharpest philosophical claim: desire is not epistemically accessible to a fully self-aware agent. You can see the trap and still walk into it. The author is directly challenging the popular therapeutic assumption that insight leads to change. Awareness might prevent action, but the desire itself - the underlying algorithm - remains unrewired by understanding.

💡 Highlight

> "The kicker is that they do this irrespective of the given individual's awareness."

The word 'kicker' signals the author knows this is the most uncomfortable point. Antagonistic valuations sabotage you WITHOUT your knowledge. This is the darker sibling to 7.19's 'lack of awareness' - there, not knowing was the problem; here, even knowing might not help. The algorithm runs below the radar of both consciousness and volition.

⚡ Discern

> "If each of our valuation algorithms for sexual relationships is principally trained on data collected from just one relationship, and this one relationship (inevitably) achieves the goals of its constituents in a suboptimal (or even dysfunctional) manner"

The word 'inevitably' is doing heavy lifting here. The author is claiming that ALL training data from a single relationship is necessarily suboptimal. But is this empirically true? Some people raised in dysfunctional households do develop functional relationship templates (sometimes negatively, by learning what NOT to do). The author's clinical experience may bias toward seeing the pathological cases. This might be overgeneralization from a particular sample.

🔗 Connect Back

> "even if she was made aware of this process, it wouldn't necessarily change her choices"

This directly inverts 7.19's 'lack of awareness' complicator. There, the problem was that people don't see their process. Here, the author shows that even full visibility into the process doesn't fix it. The 'covert calculator' runs on training data that consciousness cannot rewire. This transforms the framework from 'if only people knew their values, they'd make better choices' to something darker: the calculation is structurally immune to epistemic access.

✍️ Association

> "Her awareness might prevent her from acting"

So awareness = impulse control (preventing action) but NOT desire modification. This splits the self into: the desiring self (algorithm output) and the acting self (can optionally veto). This maps interestingly to Freud's id/superego, but with a twist: the 'superego' here isn't moralizing, it's just a veto player. And the author seems skeptical that veto power alone constitutes genuine agency.
