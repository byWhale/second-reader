Reminds me of hiring: a resume (or attraction) gets them in the door, but can they actually do the work? The author is applying a surprisingly cold, functionalist lens to something people usually treat as mystical or emotional.

🔍 "90% of a successful relationship comes down to selection" Is this number backed by any research, or is it rhetorical punch? I'd be curious whether there's empirical data on what predicts relationship satisfaction – studies often cite communication, conflict resolution, shared values – but maybe the author is arguing those are downstream of initial selection. ## Section 10.2: Three principles for selecting partners

💡 ""Just" wanting an (a) peaceful, (b) loving, and (c) committed relationship is functionally equivalent to "just" wanting an (a) high-paying, (b) low-stress job that (c) you're passionate about." The analogy lands because it exposes the gap between what we casually want and what actually exists in the world. We wouldn't call someone unrealistic for wanting all three career qualities, yet we treat the relationship version as 'settling' or 'not asking for much.' This inversion makes you recalibrate what you've been telling yourself about your own 'reasonable expectations.'

✍️ "People generally discover these essential traits by entering into relationships with people who lack them. Wisdom is pain plus insight." This echoes the experiential learning model - you don't figure out what matters by abstract reflection, you discover it through negative space, through the relationships that didn't work. There's a humility in this: it suggests your 'list' of requirements is actually a scar, not a blueprint. What you need wasn't obvious to you until you felt its absence. The phrase 'wisdom is pain plus insight' condenses something many therapy traditions say in longer form - that growth comes from integrating difficult experience, not avoiding it.

🔍 "This is another reason why I generally advise women against leading with their desire for a "serious relationship" (and why I counsel men that this is a red flag)." The author conflates two distinct things here: (1) being upfront about wanting something serious, which seems generally healthy, and (2) having a rigid preconceived structure that you'll force the other person to fit into. These seem different. I'd want to understand whether the warning is about verbal communication of intent ('I want marriage eventually') or about the mental framework itself ('I have a 5-year plan and you're either in or out'). The Procrustean analogy supports the latter, but the phrasing targets the former. Is there a distinction the author is collapsing, or do they see them as inevitably linked?

## Section 10.3: The cost of multiple criteria in dating market

💡 "it's a way of eschewing relationships in a socially acceptable way" This is the kind of line that makes you slightly uncomfortable - because it might be true about someone you know, or maybe even yourself. The social approval around being 'selective' shields it from scrutiny. The author is basically saying: some people don't need to explain their single status as 'haven't found the right person' - they can just keep adding criteria and never have to face the harder truth that they're afraid of intimacy or commitment. That's a sharp observation, and the fact that it's uncomfortable is probably a sign it's landing.

✍️ "in a city like New York, about two million single women might have their sights set on 40,000 men (who may or may not be single), which is a ratio of 50-to-1" The 50-to-1 number is visceral in a way that changes the entire framing. It's one thing to say 'there are more women than men in certain cities' - it's another to calculate that you're competing with 49 other women for every man who meets your criteria. This reframes 'why can't I find a good man?' not as a personal failure but as basic arithmetic. The author then flips this to say: and YOU are also competing with millions of other women for those same men. It almost makes the transaction model feel merciful - at least economics gives you a language for why things are hard, even if it doesn't give you a solution.

✍️ "Insisting on unnecessary criteria is the most common way people price themselves out of the marketplace. And this is due to a deficit in self-knowledge" This lands as the culmination of everything since 16.1. Back in 16.2, the author said you discover what matters through pain and failure - your 'list' is actually a scar, not a blueprint. Now he's saying: but most people never do that work, so they keep lists that are either (a) based on whatsociety says they should want, or (b) based on a fantasy of who they think they are. The self-knowledge deficit isn't just about knowing what you need - it's about knowing what you actually bring to the table. Most people overvalue their own offer and undervalue what they can realistically get. This connects to something like the Dunning-Kruger effect but in the relationship market.

🔍 "The cost of winning is overpaying" The author says that to 'win' in this marketplace (get the most desirable partner), you have to overpay - offer more than your market value would suggest. But I'm wondering: is this always true? Could there be people who find 'undervalued' matches - people who are genuinely desirable but for some reason aren't competing in the obvious marketplace (maybe they're introverted, maybe they don't broadcast their success)? Like value stocks that haven't been discovered yet. The author's frame is very transactional, but I'm not sure he accounts for information asymmetry or people who opt out of the visible market entirely.

## Section 10.4: Treating dating as hiring process with hire slow fire fast

💡 ""spouse" is a lifetime appointment (at least, in theory). In terms of the security it affords and the consequences associated with choosing poorly, it is the relationship equivalent of "pope" or "supreme court justice." As such, there is absolutely no hurry in the selection process." This is the gravitational center of the entire chapter. The inversion is sharp: we spend years vetting a job candidate and months choosing a spouse. The pope/Supreme Court justice comparison works because it identifies the actual stakes - these are positions where removal is nearly impossible and the consequences of poor selection ripple for decades. Most relationship advice says 'trust your gut' or 'follow your heart'; this says 'conduct due diligence' - and the metaphor makes that feel less cold and more obviously necessary.

✍️ "hire slow and fire fast" This phrase exists in startup/investing culture too, and there's an interesting parallel: in both contexts, people violate it constantly. Founders hold onto underperforming hires far too long because they've 'invested' in them. People stay in mismatched relationships for the same reason - they've built a life together, shared friends, etc. The 'sunk cost' bias operates identically in both professional and romantic contexts. The author is essentially saying: recognize that the same behavioral traps that wreck companies also wreck relationships, and apply the same corrective.

✍️ "the first of the three principal crises through which most couples must pass is the Crisis of Disillusionment... the honeymoon period in which these fantasies are predominant can typically last up to a year" This connects back to earlier chapters - specifically 16.2's three principles and 16.3's self-knowledge deficit argument. The author is building a coherent model: you need self-knowledge to select well (16.3), you need to understand the selection principles (16.2), and now you need to understand the timeline of actual perception vs. fantasy. The year-long honeymoon period is the mechanism that makes rushing dangerous - you're not actually choosing the real person, you're choosing your projection of them. This temporal element makes 'hire slow' concrete rather than abstract.

🔍 "Most people only get a few good shots at a successful long-term relationship in their entire lives." This claim appears frequently in relationship advice but I'd want to see the data. Is it based on marriage/divorce statistics? Longitudinal studies on relationship histories? Or is it more of a 'given typical age ranges and market dynamics' heuristic? The framing matters because it underpins the urgency of the selection argument.

## Section 10.5: Deal breakers and rejecting unsuitable prospects

💡 "This is one of the least-appreciated reasons why being attractive is important: increasing optionality affords people a better opportunity to avoid having to choose between their sexual and emotional needs and their dignity and self-respect." The reframe here is significant. Attractiveness literature typically frames 'more options' as the benefit. This flips it to dignity preservation - it's not about having choices, but about having the power to reject without accepting crumbs. It connects directly to the self-knowledge deficit in 16.3: people who don't know what they need also don't know what they can realistically get, so their perceived optionality is miscalibrated in both directions.

💡 "Opposites might attract – but they generally don't stay together." Tight, memorable phrasing that deserves to be quoted. It encapsulates the incompatibility argument without belaboring it.

💡 "Incompatible relationships require more resources (such as time, attention, patience, and creativity) than relationships without such differences – resources that might be better spent in the service of what the relationship was designed to accomplish (as opposed to simply maintaining the relationship itself)." This is the economic argument for compatibility, and it's more subtle than typical advice. Most relationship discourse focuses on 'working through differences' without quantifying the cost. The author is saying: even when incompatibility is 'managed,' you're spending resources on maintenance rather than growth. That's a cost that's invisible until you name it. The parallel to 16.4's hiring metaphor is clean - you're not just paying for the employee's salary, you're paying for the management overhead.

✍️ "Demanding that he or she change after the fact is the relationship equivalent of buyer's remorse." This connects to earlier segments: 16.2's principle that you choose people based on what they actually do, not what they promise; 16.4's 'hire those who already have the traits.' The buyer's remorse analogy works because it exposes the fundamental error - you've made the selection, now you're trying to undo the selection through leverage rather than accepting that you picked someone whose faults you need to be able to live with. It also echoes the 'you can't have any relationship with anyone, only certain relationships with certain people' from earlier - the person either can give you what you need or they can't; demanding change is an admission that your initial selection was wrong.

🔍 "Finally, to the extent that optionality is associated with attractiveness, most women should never be pickier – and most men should never be less picky – than they are today." The author asserts this without data or argument - it lands as a controversial take without justification. Is this empirical? Heuristic? The previous sections built careful, logical arguments, then this final line feels like it's picking a fight rather than making a point. I'd want to see what evidence exists for gender-differentiated pickiness norms, or whether this is simply 'provocative statement for its own sake.'

## Section 10.6: Relationship investment continuum and role definition

💡 "Women can be the employers or they can get a paycheck, but they can't have it both ways" This is the argumentative apex of the segment, and it deserves scrutiny rather than acceptance. The author presents this as a logical derivation from hypergamy, but the move from 'women prefer higher-status men' to 'women are employees who take money' requires premises the text doesn't establish. Hypergamy could just as easily describe a marketplace where women are selective consumers rather than wage-earners. The framing treats an outcome (resource flow from man to woman) as evidence of a structural role, when it could equally be evidence of negotiated terms within a specific relationship. The phrase 'can't have it both ways' sounds decisive, but it's actually doing something sly: it's defining 'power' narrowly enough that any counterexample can be dismissed as trying to have it both ways.

✍️ "women's role as employees in the sexual marketplace is not the vestige of oppressive patriarchal social structures: it is a necessary consequence of their hypergamous tendencies" This connects to something the author himself might not intend: the long history of treating economic arrangements as 'natural' rather than constructed. The phrase 'not the vestige of oppressive structures, but a necessary consequence' is doing ideological work - it takes a contested social arrangement and presents it as discovered rather than chosen. This is the same move economists sometimes make when they treat existing market distributions as efficient outcomes rather than power settlements. The author's confidence here contrasts sharply with his earlier empirical caution about things like the 50-to-1 ratio, which he presented as a calculation. This feels more like a conclusion in search of justification than a finding derived from evidence.

🔍 "women sometimes rankle at this comparison... this aligns with the intentions of most women in the sexual marketplace" The author acknowledges resistance but dismisses it in a single sentence - 'women sometimes rankle' - before asserting his framework actually captures their 'intentions.' This is a notable move: he's claiming to know women's 'real' intentions better than women themselves experience them. I'd want to see what evidence exists for this claim. Is there research showing women generally view themselves as employees in the sexual marketplace? Survey data? Evolutionary psychology literature? Or is this an inference from the model itself (if the model is correct, then women's intentions must align with it)? This feels like where the argument is weakest - not in its descriptive power, but in its presumptuous access to interiority.

## Section 10.7: Gender differences in criteria and marketplace approach

💡 "if you don't have a plan, it means that you're part of someone else's" This echoes 16.4's hiring framework but inverts the agency dynamic - not having criteria doesn't just fail you, it makes you a resource in someone else's strategy. That's a sharp reversal of the 'you are the employer' framing from earlier sections.

💡 "Women can conflate a man's sexual interest for his willingness to commit because this is how they themselves tend to navigate selection." This is the analytical centerpiece - the author is saying women misread men's signals because they're projecting their own selection logic. It's an elegant explanation for a common relationship dynamic where women wait for 'the talk' that never comes. But the confidence with which this is stated ('because this is how they themselves tend to navigate selection') assumes access to women's interior reasoning that feels presumptuous.

✍️ "men generally set the bar for a casual relationship very low, which is why most men will have sex with most women, and the bar for a serious relationship very high" This connects to something real in dating dynamics but gets stated as descriptive fact. The asymmetry is: for men, casual is easy/serious is hard; for women, casual is hard/serious is hard. The author treats this as evolutionary inevitability, but it could also be read as culturally constructed gender scripts that self-reinforce. The Coolidge effect reference anchors this in evolutionary psychology, but invoking it as explanation for 'other observed differences in gendered sexuality' feels like using one contested concept to explain another.

🔍 "the information needed to evaluate a potential candidate must be obtained indirectly" The 'covert evaluation' instruction feels like a rhetorical flourish that doesn't survive contact with practice. If you can't directly ask about someone's values, what does indirect evaluation actually look like? And at what point does 'covert evaluation' become manipulation or testing? ## Section 10.8: Indirect methods for evaluating candidates

💡 "This is why smart companies don't hire using structured interviews (at least, not exclusively). Instead, they utilize many different methods to indirectly collect the information they need... People might not tell you who they are, but they will show you who they are – if you give them the opportunity." This segment earns its place primarily through the cohabitation warning and the 'test drive' metaphor. The author makes a sharp point: once you've moved in with someone, you've already made the serious commitment - the evaluation period is over. Using cohabitation as a 'test' defeats the purpose because the stakes are now too high to exit cleanly. This connects cleanly to 'hire slow fire fast' from 16.4 - the test drive should happen before you buy. The indirect evaluation advice is more familiar, but the framing that people 'show' rather than 'tell' who they are is a useful reframe that connects to earlier points about not trusting what people promise.

## Section 10.9: Two key self-reflection questions for vetting

💡 "the mind cannot decide... It can't tell you what you should do or whether something will be worth doing. It can only crunch the numbers according to the parameters defined by your experience and intention." This is the philosophical hinge of the entire chapter. The author concedes that the rational 'hiring' framework from 16.4-16.8 has real limits - the mind can't actually make decisions, only calculate probabilities. This is a surprisingly honest admission that elevates the argument. Most self-help books would double down on rationality; here, he's acknowledging its boundary while also rejecting pure emotionalism. The framing of the mind as an 'odds calculator' rather than a decision-maker is genuinely useful - it explains why we can have all the data and still feel stuck.

✍️ "Decision is ultimately an act of the will – which, as such, makes it closer to an emotion since its function is to motivate action." This connects to William James's philosophy of will - James argued that willing isn't primarily intellectual but involves emotional commitment that moves us to action. It also echoes existentialist themes (Sartre's 'commitment' as defining oneself) and Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis in neuroscience - that emotion is necessary for practical decision-making, not a distortion of rational choice. The author is making a philosophical claim that decisions are more like 'choosing to want' than 'computing the optimal answer,' which feels right phenomenologically even if the neuroscience is more complicated.

✍️ "This is why it is odd that we collectively do all that we can to sacrifice this attraction on the altar of security in our long-term relationships." This is a sharp observation that inverts typical relationship advice. We tell people attraction will 'grow' or that it's 'shallow' to prioritize it, then wonder why long-term relationships become resentful. The author correctly identifies that attraction drives the willingness to sacrifice and serve - without it, the 'security' we prioritize becomes hollow. This directly challenges the 'settle for someone who's a good partner even if you're not that attracted' advice that gets given to people (especially women).

This feels like a genuine cliffhanger - the author is about to give us the practical emotional framework for selection and then... nothing. What are these two questions?
