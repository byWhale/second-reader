# Prologue

## Section 1: Introduction to the story behind the cover painting / Eris arrives at the wedding and causes discord

💡 "inevitably showed up anyway" This phrase quietly does a lot of work. It frames discord as not just present but cosmically inevitable - you can invite every god and goddess except the one who represents chaos, and still, she will come. There's something darkly comedic about the inevitability.

✍️ "Wisely perceiving that there was no way this couldn't end poorly for him" The authorial voice here has a wry, modern sensibility - 'no way this couldn't end poorly' reads like someone narrating a disaster movie. It signals this book won't be a dry retelling but will have a point of view. This tone reminds me of how Neil Gaiman often approaches mythology - with affection but also clear-eyed about the absurdity.

🔍 "There is a story behind the painting on the cover of this book" The book is explicitly framing itself around a specific painting. What painting is this? Which artist? The choice to open with 'the story behind the painting' suggests the book will use art history as a lens - probably to examine how this myth has been visualized and what those visual choices reveal.

## Section 2: Three goddesses bribe Paris with gifts / The judgment leads to the Trojan War

💡 "Whether Helen was kidnapped or she followed Paris of her own accord is a detail that changes depending on who is telling the story." This single sentence quietly does something remarkable: it acknowledges that mythology has no neutral ground. The 'detail' isn't just a plot point—it's a choice about who deserves sympathy, who is villain and who is victim. The author leaves it deliberately unresolved, which is itself a statement.

✍️ "But a promise is a promise." The casualness of this phrase against the backdrop of a ten-year war and a city's destruction creates a darkly comic effect. It echoes the earlier 'no way this couldn't end poorly'—both are narratorial winks that acknowledge the absurdity while telling the story straight. This tone reminds me of how Ursula K. Le Guin retold myths: with affection, but also with a clear awareness that these aren't just charming old tales—they're political narratives that shaped civilizations.

🔍 "Unfortunately for all involved" This phrase is doing something interesting—it's simultaneously empathetic (acknowledging all the suffering) and detached (treating it as an understatement). Is this playful distance meant to make the ancient story feel accessible to modern readers, or is there a more critical intention? The book seems to be building a consistent narratorial personality, but I'm not yet sure where it's leading.
