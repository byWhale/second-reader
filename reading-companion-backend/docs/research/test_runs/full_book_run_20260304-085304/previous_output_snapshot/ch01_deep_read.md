## Section 1.2: Clarifying prosocial/antisocial and relationship opportunities

This is the kind of etymological insight that makes the whole argument feel inevitable rather than constructed. It's not cynicism—it's built into the language.

💡

This is stark. The author is deliberately provocative here, stripping away moral language from relationship formation. ## Section 1.3: Core definition: relationship exists where value is transacted

💡

> "the media in which unequal goods of comparable value are exchanged"

This refined definition is doing something the earlier 'value transacted' framing couldn't: it explains why relationships aren't just transactions but something more complicated. The asymmetry is key—you give me something I can't give myself, I give you something you can't give yourself, and neither of us could just generate what the other provides. 'Comparable' doesn't mean equal in any objective sense; it means each party subjectively values what they receive at least as much as what they give. This is the paradox at the heart of all relationships: you want what you don't have, from someone who doesn't have what you have, and somehow this creates a bond.

💡

> "relationships must be negotiated – not just at their inception but through their entire duration, as well"

This is a crucial expansion. Most relationship theories treat formation as the hard part and maintenance as automatic. This framework says no—every ongoing relationship is a continuous negotiation of value, which explains why relationships are fragile and require work. The valuation isn't fixed; as circumstances change, what you need shifts, and the 'deal' must be renegotiated.

💡

> "the transaction of value in such relationships is typically negotiated covertly: it must be approached with subtlety, tact, and indirectness"

The author anticipates the objection here and frames it as a social convention rather than a refutation. The 'romantic ideal' that relationships 'just happen' through pure goodwill is itself a negotiation strategy—it would be unseemly to explicitly state what you want from someone. This is meta-clever: the very resistance to the transactional model is itself evidence of the model working, because people want to believe they're motivated by pure altruism (which is more valued in the social marketplace).

✍️

> "value is neither static nor objective... it exists solely in the mind of the valuer"

This connects to behavioral economics's concept of 'framing effects' and 'anchoring'—the same object is valued differently depending on context, mood, and comparison points. What the author adds is that this incalculability is *necessary* for relationships to exist; if value were objective and calculable, transactions would be simple exchanges rather than relationships. The 'mystery' of what the other truly values is what creates the relational space.

🔍

> "this valuation typically occurs beneath the threshold of awareness"

What is the actual mechanism by which people assess comparable value without conscious calculation? Is this something like an emotional 'gut check'—a feeling of whether the exchange 'feels right'? And if it's below awareness, how do people develop skill at it? Can you get 'better' at relationships the same way you develop intuitions in other domains through pattern recognition?

## Section 1.4: Relationships as games with rules and laws

💡

> "Rules are more culturally informed and can be broken (though often not without interpersonal consequence). Laws are more biologically determined and are much more difficult to break (and generally not without intrapersonal consequence)."

The distinction is clean, but I'm not convinced 'biologically determined' is established rather than asserted. The author is making a strong empirical claim about universality—that these 'laws' function like biological invariants—but offers no evidence. This feels like the kind of framing that sounds scientific without being demonstrated.

💡

> "If people get too little of what they want (or too much of what they don't want), they stop playing – which, incidentally, is another relationship law."

This is doing real work: it frames relationship dissolution as an automatic exit from the 'game' when payoffs become unfavorable. But there's an unstated question—what's the threshold? Is it subjective, calculable, or purely emotional? The author gestures at a mechanism without specifying it.

✍️

> "A game is anything with rules and a goal."

The author preemptively defends against 'game' sounding trivial by noting that some games are played for very high stakes. This is a reasonable move, but it also reveals something: the very fact that people resist this framing suggests the transactional model hits a cultural nerve. We want relationships to be about something 'purer' than value exchange. The resistance IS the data—the author essentially acknowledges that we've collectively agreed to hide the transaction beneath romance, friendship, generosity. The cover-up proves the thing exists.

🔍

> "Relationship laws are fairly constant and universal."

How would we actually test this? What would count as evidence that a 'relationship law' is universal across cultures and time periods? Anthropological literature on kinship systems, gift economies (Mauss's The Gift), and cross-cultural variation in relationship structures seems like it would either support or complicate this claim.

## Section 1.6: Economic model of relationships: behavioral economics

💡

> "people enter into (and remain in) sexual relationships with their perceived best options"

This is the fundamental law the author is building around. It's essentially applying comparative advantage to mating: you choose the best available, not the objectively best. This quietly acknowledges that choice is always contextual and constrained by what's actually available - not some Platonic ideal of the perfect partner.

💡

> "the model can explain emotions (but emotions cannot explain the model), this means that the model is more fundamentally true and should take precedence"

This is the passage's most ambitious and simultaneously most questionable claim. The author conflates 'underlying mechanism' with 'more true explanation' - just because value calculations may causally produce emotions doesn't mean emotional reasons are illusory or should be discarded when explaining behavior. This is a category error between causal explanation and motivating reason. When someone says 'I stayed because I loved them,' the emotional statement is not refuted by 'actually you stayed because the perceived value exceeded your threshold.' Both can be simultaneously true, and the author's claim to 'precedence' is philosophical overreach, not logical necessity.

✍️

> "behavioral economics"

The author is essentially claiming that mating is a domain where Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky) applies - people are loss-averse, frame outcomes relative to reference points (their alternatives), and systematically deviate from 'rational' calculation in predictable ways. The 'perceived best option' framing maps onto satisficing (Simon) rather than optimizing - we choose who seems good enough among what we can actually get, not the theoretical best. This behavioral economics lens treats mating like a market with imperfect information and bounded rationality.

🔍

> "most people either don't understand or are (at best) only dimly aware of the game they are playing"

If most people are 'dimly aware' of this game, how did the author come to understand it? Is this a case of the model being internally coherent but not actually discoverable through introspection? This creates a strange epistemic position: the model explains behavior that actors themselves cannot report on. This mirrors the 'unconscious competence' problem - the author claims expert knowledge of a game that players can't consciously describe. How would we verify or falsify this framework if its core claim is that its truth is hidden from participants?

## Section 1.8: The covert calculator: unconscious valuation process

💡

> "This is because the vast majority of this process occurs well below the threshold of awareness: individuals are generally cognizant of only a few aspects of the calculation, at best. And this can be off-putting, as it suggests not only that people are not aware of why they value the things they do but that their valuations might be influenced without their knowledge, as well."

This is the most honest sentence in the section. The author explicitly acknowledges the uncomfortable implication: we don't know why we want what we want, and we can be manipulated without knowing it. This is precisely what makes the "romantic ideal" so appealing - the fiction that we choose based on genuine feeling rather than unconscious calculation. The author essentially says 'yes, it's creepy, but it's true.'

🔍

> "This tendency is universal: there is no culture in which it doesn't exist. This is because the principles of valuation are like the principles of logic: they do not change from place to place or moment to moment."

This universality claim directly contradicts what behavioral economics has documented - systematic, predictable deviations from rational choice that ARE universal ( Prospect Theory). The fact that humans universally make the 'same mistakes' suggests the 'principles' aren't like logic at all. If valuation were hardwired like logic, why do we systematically violate expected utility theory?

This resolves the apparent conflict between 'emotional reasons' and 'rational calculation' by showing they're the same process at different levels of access.

This trio of examples is doing real work—showing the gradient from urgent need to aesthetic recognition to affect. ## Section 1.10: Applying value to people: exemplars and comparisons

💡

> "one of our most common decision-making heuristics is to (often unconsciously) determine the perceived goodness of fit between any specific candidate and its relevant exemplar"

This is the key move: we're not calculating raw utility, we're matching against internalized templates. This reframes the 'covert calculator' from 7.8 - it's not adding up value points but comparing against an idealized prototype. But this raises a nested question: if the exemplar itself was formed through past valuations, aren't we just deferring the mechanism one layer back?

💡

> "the bi-directional relationship between exemplars and selection helps us in this process. That is, because I know your exemplar, I can predict that you'll choose to enter into a relationship with a specific person... And because you've chosen to enter into a relationship with a certain individual... I can deduce"

This is methodologically clever but potentially circular. Exemplar → predicts choice / choice → reveals exemplar. It's a closed interpretive loop that could explain anything post-hoc but has weak predictive power. The author treats this as a feature ('Fortunately, the bi-directional relationship helps us'), but it reads more like the soft underbelly of the framework - exactly the kind of unfalsifiable structure that makesPopper squirm.

✍️

> "What problem of living are they attempting to solve?"

This phrase carries echoes of Heidegger's 'living' (Dasein) and the existentialist tradition, but functionally it's just 'what goal are they pursuing?' The rhetorical flourish doesn't add explanatory power. More substantively, this connects to the emotional translation thread from 7.9: if relationships solve problems of living, and emotions are translated valuations - then love might literally be the feeling of a problem being solved. That's either profound or just relabeling.

🔍

> "we also seem to possess exemplars – or idealized constructs – associated with specific types of relationship partners"

Where do these exemplars actually come from? The book leaves this blank. Are they formed through early attachment (Bowlby)? Cultural templates (social learning)? Accumulated negative prototypes from past disappointments? The model needs this but doesn't specify it - which makes the whole exemplar-matching heuristic hard to test empirically.

## Section 1.11: Mating as game of games: evolutionary genetic survival

💡

> "While the 'game of war' is associated with the survival of the individual organism, the 'game of love' is associated with the survival of the individual's genes. From an evolutionary standpoint, an organism that lives to a ripe, old age without reproducing not only dies – it goes extinct."

This is the evolutionary 'why' that's been missing. Previous segments established that value depends on goal hierarchy position, but didn't explain WHY genetic survival would outrank individual survival. The author's answer is sharp: an organism that doesn't reproduce has effectively 'gone extinct' from an evolutionary perspective - genetic survival is the deeper game. The 'goes extinct' phrasing is deliberately stark.

✍️

> "A mother who dies so her children might live exemplifies this phenomenon."

This connects directly to evolutionary biology's kin selection theory (Hamilton's rule) and inclusive fitness. The author's framing - 'genetic survival is a more important goal than individual survival for perhaps all living beings' - is essentially describing how natural selection operates at the gene level, not the organism level (Dawkins' 'selfish gene' perspective). It's a bold universal claim that would need empirical support, but it does the philosophical work of explaining why mating sits at the top of the hierarchy.

💡

> "the game of mating and dating ranks very highly in most people's nested hierarchy of games – in many cases, above their own individual survival. In many respects, it is the game of games: the game that makes all other games possible."

This is the payoff sentence for the entire argument. 'Game of games' (meta-game) is the right framing - it explains why people tolerate behavior in mating contexts they wouldn't accept elsewhere, why the sexual marketplace is 'functionally everywhere,' and why desperate/hurtful actions happen. The stakes aren't just personal - they're genomic.

💡

> "A starving person is likely not proud of choosing to steal food – and we probably wouldn't uphold the behavior as an exemplar for ethical conduct – but we would almost certainly understand this person's decision."

This is the key bridge: understanding doesn't equal endorsing. The author uses this to pre-empt moral objections to the model. We're allowed to understand why people do desperate things in the mating market without celebrating those behaviors. It's a smart rhetorical move.

💡

> "Think of a plumber... no one continues to pay the plumber after the job is finished."

The plumber analogy is the crystallizing image for the instrumentality principle from earlier segments. It makes visceral something abstract: value isn't intrinsic to the person, it's about what they can do for you. The 'balk at this' acknowledgment shows the author knows this sounds cold - but the logic is relentless.

🔍

> "As a consequence, the game of mating and dating ranks very highly in most people's nested hierarchy of games"

If genetic survival is THE superordinate evolutionary goal, why do some people consciously choose not to reproduce? Does the model account for goals that conflict with evolutionary logic - or does it reclassify those goals as lower in the hierarchy? Also: what about gay individuals who may not reproduce but still have strong mating drives - how does the model handle this?

## Section 1.12: Value fluctuation and high-value individuals, desire

💡

> "value and desire are the same thing experienced in different ways"

The author names the tautology explicitly and frames it as 'unsatisfying'—but this is precisely what makes it powerful. Most frameworks would hide the circularity; this one names it. It means there's no separate 'love feeling' distinct from the valuation—the feeling IS the valuation. This resolves the age-old 'choice vs. feeling' debate by dissolving it: there's no 'choice' to act on that isn't already colored by the desire that arose from the valuation.

💡

> "A husband who was willing to forgo an attractive sexual opportunity for the sake of a woman's maternal instincts will likely be less willing to do so once his children are grown up... she will have become less valuable to him for having given him what he wanted"

This is the brutal crystallization of the 'plumber' analogy from 7.11. The husband doesn't consciously think 'her utility has dropped'—he just feels less attracted. And the guilt comes from sensing this discrepancy: she gave him what he wanted, and now he wants her less. The author calls this 'cruel reality.' It's not that the husband is evil—it's that the value calculus shifted, and the emotion followed.

✍️

> "desire is the natural emotional response to the perception of high value"

This connects back to 7.9's translation mechanism: value → emotion. But now we see domain-specificity: in sexual relationships, the translated emotion is specifically desire (not, say, admiration or gratitude). The 'game of games' framing from 7.11 makes this make sense—genetic survival is the ultimate game, so the valuation translates into the most urgent drive. Desire isn't 'romantic love'—it's the operational signal for genetic investment.

💡

> "The more a particular person is perceived to be valuable... the more this particular person will be desired by the individual in question"

The parenthetical 'as prioritized in that specific moment' does enormous work. It admits that value is entirely moment-dependent. Your wife isn't 'objectively' less valuable—the same traits exist, but their instrumentality to your current goals has shifted. This is the mechanism behind why relationships feel unstable even when nothing 'objectively' changed.

🔍

> "saying people desire a high-value individual is a tautology"

If desire IS value perception (and not caused by it), what does this mean for the question of whether love is a choice or a feeling? The model says they're not separate—there's no 'love' beyond the valuation. But this raises the nested problem: if we can become aware of this mechanism, can we intervene in it? Can we choose to value someone differently, or is the translation one-way?

## Section 1.13: Lack of awareness: unconscious valuation problems

💡

> "This causes people to (rationally) undervalue potential future liabilities, which then contributes to tipping their perception of any given individual in the direction of a net positive."

This is the mechanism for why warning signs get dismissed. The 'rationally' in parentheses is doing important work—it acknowledges that this isn't a cognitive error but a mathematically consistent application of probability weighting. Since there's a chance the liability won't materialize, we discount it. The paradox is that this perfectly rational calculation produces irrational-seeming outcomes (ignoring red flags).

💡

> "by weighting values associated with current benefits more heavily, and weighting values associated with future liabilities more lightly. This is in the service of motivating action in alignment with their valuations."

This is the crucial second-order effect: desire doesn't just RESULT from valuation—it actively distorts the weighting function that produced it. It's a feedback loop. The emotion generated by high current value changes the very calculator that generated it. This explains why 'you can't think clearly when you're attracted to someone' isn't just metaphor—it's literal: the emotional state changes the computational weights.

💡

> "This reciprocal relationship between the perception of value and desire explains why attractive people tend to receive all kinds of allowances that less attractive people don't – including the tendency to discount or minimize the implications of their bad behavior."

This is the uncomfortable application. The 'allowances' aren't arbitrary favoritism—they're the predictable outcome of the feedback loop. An attractive person generates more desire, which further distorts their valuation (more weight on current benefits, less on future liabilities). This isn't moral judgment; it's the model working as designed. It also explains why accountability is harder in dating than in other contexts—the emotional stakes literally distort perception.

💡

> "if the process of determining value is comprised of a countless number of individual evaluations, each associated with a specific weight that attempts to predict the importance of that evaluation to the achievement of a particular goal, and if the valuation process is almost entirely unconscious, then (to a large extent) people will be unaware of precisely what they value and the extent to which they value those things."

This is the 'model fails' pivot—the author is now saying the unconscious calculator (from 7.8) is not just unknown to us in its operations, but its OUTPUT (what we actually value) is also hidden from conscious access. We know our stated preferences, but those are post-hoc rationalizations of choices whose real drivers we're unaware of.

💡

> "A woman who consciously thinks she wants a nice man to settle down with but who constantly finds herself getting involved in short-term relationships with exploitative bad boys is completely in the dark with respect to her actual goals and values, which are revealed by examining her choices."

This is the devastating illustration. The gap between stated preference ('I want a nice man') and revealed preference (choosing bad boys) isn't hypocrisy—it's genuine unawareness. She might 'vigorously deny' having a type because she genuinely doesn't know. The author explicitly connects this to the exemplar framework from 7.10: her unconscious exemplar is misaligned with her conscious goals.

💡

> "Just like a job interviewer can't be completely transparent about the actual criteria for selection – as doing so would significantly increase the likelihood that candidates would simply tell the interviewer what she wants to hear – people are generally forced into some measure of dissimulation when trying to get what they want from the opposite sex."

The job interviewer analogy crystallizes why direct questioning fails. Both parties have incentives to obscure: candidates lie, daters hide their actual criteria because disclosure would compromise negotiation. This means dating is structurally a game of asymmetric information—which connects back to the 'game of games' framing from 7.11.

💡

> "what people are attracted to is not always rationally aligned with their best interests (or even their consciously explicated goals). This is due to the fact that – while the valuation process is inherent – the weights and evaluations used in that process are not."

This is the key distinction the author is setting up: valuation capacity is biological (we can't help but value), but the specific weights and evaluations are acquired (shaped by perception, culture, experience). This means while the process is unconscious, it's not fixed—we can potentially examine and revise what we value, even if the raw capacity is unchangeable.

✍️

> "the valuation process is almost entirely unconscious... people will be unaware of precisely what they value"

This connects to the earlier segment (7.9) about the 'honest sentence'—the author acknowledged that this model implies we can be 'influenced without our knowledge.' Now he's extending that: we don't just not know WHY we value things, we don't know WHAT we value (as shown by the behavior-revealed-preference gap). This is a deeper level of the same uncomfortable implication. The romantic ideal (we follow our genuine feelings) becomes even more fictionally appealing when you realize the feelings themselves are opaque to us.

✍️

> "Just like a job interviewer can't be completely transparent about the actual criteria"

This brings back the plumber analogy from 7.11—relationships as instrumental. The interviewer analogy extends this: not only is value instrumental, but the ADMISSION of instrumental criteria is strategically hidden. In hiring, we pretend 'cultural fit' means something other than 'will this person make my life easier.' In dating, we pretend attraction is 'mysterious' rather than 'I calculated your utility to my goals.' Both are games of strategic concealment.

🔍

> "This causes people to (rationally) undervalue potential future liabilities"

The author says this is 'rational' undervaluation—is this actually rational, or is it a rationalization? There's a large literature on temporal discounting showing that humans systematically overweight present rewards and underweight future costs. We call it 'rational' when it follows the mathematical logic of probability, but behavioral economics would say our baseline discounting is already distorted relative to true 'rational' agent models.

💡

> "To some extent and without exception, all people utilize algorithms that contain valuations that are either irrelevant or antagonistic to their goals."

The 'without exception' is doing enormous rhetorical work here. It's bold - maybe too bold. But it serves a purpose: it universalizes the problem so no one can comfort themselves with 'that doesn't apply to me.' The author wants to say: you're not broken, you're just running corrupted software - and so is everyone else.

💡

> "Irrelevant valuations are liabilities because they reduce the pool of satisfactory options... Focusing on irrelevant criteria can also distract you from more important considerations. However, antagonistic valuations are even more problematic because they effectively sabotage goal-directed efforts."

The distinction between 'irrelevant' (passive constraint) and 'antagonistic' (active harm) is the structural payoff of this entire section. Irrelevant valuations are annoying - you waste time, spend more resources. Antagonistic valuations are dangerous - they make you actively choose against your own interests while feeling like you're choosing FOR them. The 'kicker' that this happens 'irrespective of the given individual's awareness' is the dagger.

✍️

This connects to something every therapist knows: people reproduce their parents' marriage in disturbingly precise ways - not because they want to, but because their algorithm was trained on that data. The 'sparrow exemplar' image (trained on one sparrow, can't recognize penguins as birds) is the mechanism behind the intergenerational transmission of relational dysfunction - and also of relational patterns that are merely 'different' but not 'bad.' ## Section 1.15: Chapter conclusion and transition to sexual marketplace

💡

> "the most effective way to correct them is to flood these algorithms with better data... Rather, the idea is to expose people to so much good data that the bad data on which they were trained are rendered increasingly irrelevant."

This is the chapter's key intervention - the practical solution to the 'antagonistic valuations' problem raised in earlier segments. The author makes a smart move here: instead of promising to 'erase' bad training data (which would require memory manipulation and raises Orwellian issues), he proposes dilution through overwhelming exposure. The image is neural-network-like: bad weights get drowned out by new, better-weighted examples. This answers the implicit 'how do we actually deviate from corrupted algorithm outputs?' question that was left open.

💡

> "While our desires may not entirely be our fault, they remain our responsibilities. However, it's my hope that this understanding may open up new opportunities to relate to each other more humanely."

This is the crucial ethical framing. The author refuses both horns of a false dilemma: we're not blameworthy for our trained algorithms (so no point in guilt), but we're also not excused from accountability (so no 'it's just my programming' escape hatch). The 'relate more humanely' hope is interesting - understanding the mechanics doesn't just give us optimization strategies, it gives us compassion for the human condition of running corrupted software we didn't choose.

💡

> "we can truly only love to the depth of our understanding."

This is the closing flourish, and it's doing double duty rhetorically. But here's the thing: earlier the author established that love/desire/valuation are the same thing experienced differently. If that's true, then 'love to the depth of our understanding' is almost tautological - we can only value to the depth of our (valuation) understanding. It sounds profound, but it might just be a rhetorical bow on top of a relabeling exercise. That said, in the context of the chapter's argument, it also functions as a humility constraint: our love is limited by our (often corrupted) perceptual apparatus.

✍️

> "expose people to so much good data that the bad data on which they were trained are rendered increasingly irrelevant"

This 'flood' mechanism connects to exposure therapy in clinical psychology - you don't delete the fear response, you create new (incompatible) associations that eventually overwhelm the old ones. It also echoes the neural network concept of 'overfitting' - if your model was trained on biased data, you need new data to generalize better. The author seems to be arguing that relationships themselves are the retraining environment. But there's a practical problem: for someone deeply embedded in a dysfunctional relational ecosystem, where do the 'better exemplars' come from? The solution assumes access to better data, which may be precisely what the problem population lacks.

✍️

> "the most effective way to correct them is to flood these algorithms with better data"

This directly resolves the open thread from earlier segments: if bad exemplars train bad algorithms (7.14), then good exemplars can retrain them. The circularity is interesting - the same exemplar-matching mechanism that CAUSED the problem becomes the proposed solution. You're not overcoming your valuation system, you're overriding it from within using the same machinery. It's like using fire to fight fire, except the fire is your own pattern-matching.

🔍

> "expose people to so much good data that the bad data on which they were trained are rendered increasingly irrelevant"

How exactly does this 'better data' intervention work in practice? Is passive exposure enough (movies, observing happy couples), or does it require active relational engagement? What happens when someone is trapped in an environment where good exemplars are inaccessible - the solution seems to require the very thing the problem creates obstacles to.
